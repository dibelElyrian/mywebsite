Weak WiFi is a common frustration in small homes and apartments. The signal drops, videos buffer, and calls break up. The fix is often simple and low cost. This guide covers the usual causes, quick fixes you can do today, and a few budget product options if you need new hardware.

## Step 1: Fix placement before buying anything

WiFi routers need open space. A router placed in a cabinet or behind a TV can lose signal fast.

- Place the router on a shelf or table, not on the floor.
- Keep it away from thick walls, metal shelves, and large appliances.
- If possible, place it near the center of your home.

Even a small change in position can improve signal strength.

## Step 2: Reduce interference

Other devices can interfere with WiFi, especially in busy areas.

- Move the router away from microwaves and Bluetooth speakers.
- If you have a dual band router, use the 5GHz band for faster speeds near the router.
- Keep the 2.4GHz band for rooms farther away.

## Step 3: Check the router age

Older routers may not handle newer devices well.

- If your router is more than five years old, it may struggle with multiple users.
- Older models can also have weaker antennas.

If you have frequent drops even after moving the router, a basic upgrade can help.

## Step 4: Decide between a router and an extender

- **Router upgrade** is best if the current unit is old or unstable.
- **Range extender** is best if the router is fine but one room is weak.

If only one room has weak signal, an extender can be the cheaper fix.

## Step 5: Check basic router settings

You do not need to be a network expert. A few settings can help.

- Give your WiFi a clear name so you can tell the 2.4GHz and 5GHz bands apart.
- If your router allows it, set the 2.4GHz channel to a less crowded option.
- Restart the router once in a while to clear memory issues.

These steps will not fix every issue, but they can reduce random drops.

## Step 6: Separate WiFi problems from internet plan limits

Slow speeds can come from the internet plan, not the WiFi signal. Run a speed test next to the router. If speeds are low even close to the router, the issue may be the plan or the modem.

If speeds are good near the router but poor in another room, that is a WiFi coverage problem. In that case, an extender or better placement is more helpful than upgrading your plan.

## Step 7: Check device limits and updates

Budget routers can struggle if too many devices are connected at once. Disconnect unused devices and see if performance improves. If your router has a settings page, check for firmware updates. Some updates improve stability and coverage.

## When a mesh system is not necessary

Mesh systems are useful in large homes, but they are often overkill for small spaces. If your weak area is only one room away, a basic extender is usually enough. Start small and upgrade later if you still have dead zones.

## Simple speed check you can do now

Use a speed test app in two spots. Test near the router and in the weakest room. If the speed drops sharply, you likely need better placement or an extender.

## Cabling matters more than people expect

If your router uses a damaged or loose cable, the connection can be unstable. Replace old Ethernet cables and make sure the modem to router connection is secure. A small cable issue can look like a WiFi problem.
